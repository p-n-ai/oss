# Bab 6: Persamaan Linear (Linear Equations) — Teaching Notes

## Overview
Alright, now that they know what algebraic expressions and variables are, it's time to build the most critical tool in algebra: the equation! "Persamaan Linear" (Linear Equations) is all about balance. This chapter is a big one — it scales their understanding from basic one-variable equations to mapping linear relationships with *two* variables, and finally solving real-world puzzles using simultaneous linear equations.

## Prerequisites Check
- A solid grasp of forming algebraic expressions from word problems (F1-05 Ungkapan Algebra).
- An understanding of basic arithmetic operations, especially opposite operations (addition vs subtraction, multiplication vs division).
- Familiarity with the equal sign "$=$" meaning "the same as" or showing a balanced relationship, not just "the answer is".
- Basic knowledge of plotting points on a Cartesian coordinate system (crucial for 6.2 and 6.3).

## Teaching Sequence & Strategy

### 1. Recognising and Forming One-Variable Equations (15 min)
*Start your lesson by putting a physical (or drawn) balancing scale on the board. Explain that an equation (persamaan) is exactly like this scale — the left side must carry the exact same weight as the right side.*
- **Strategies:** Introduce the general form $ax + b = c$. Explain that "linear" means our variable has a highest power of 1 (no $x^2$ or $x^3$ here!). Have them shout out if it's a linear equation in one variable or not (e.g., $x+5=8$ is a YES, $x^2+1=6$ is a NO).
- **Check for Understanding (CFU):** Ask: "Is $m + 2 = 10$ a linear equation? How about $m^2 = 4$?" (Wait for their answer before confirming).
- **The Trap:** Thinking expressions are equations. If they write $x-8$, remind them an equation *must* have an equal sign: $x-8=2$.

### 2. Solving One-Variable Equations (25 min)
*Teach them that solving (menyelesaikan) just means finding the "root of the equation" (punca persamaan) that makes the balanced statement true.*
- **Strategies:** Introduce all three methods. 1. *Kaedah Cuba Jaya* (Trial and Improvement): Testing values one by one. 2. *Kaedah Pematahbalikan* (Backtracking): Reversing operations like a backwards story. 3. *Aplikasi Konsep Kesamaan* (Equality Concept): Whatever you do to one side, you MUST do to the exact same thing to the other side.
- **Check for Understanding (CFU):** Ask: "If we have $x - 4 = 10$, and we want to leave $x$ alone, what should we do to both sides?" (Wait for them to answer: Add 4).
- **The Trap:** In the formal balance method, students often subtract a number from one side but forget the other, tipping the scale. Force them to write $-3$ under *both* sides of the equation.

### 3. Expanding to Two Variables & Graphing (35 min)
*Now, let's level up to situations with two unknown quantities. Give an example: "The difference between two numbers is 18" ($p - q = 18$).*
- **Strategies:** Show them that two-variable equations have *many* pairs of solutions. Build a table of values for $x - y = -2$, find 3 or 4 points, and plot them on a grid. Show that they form a perfect straight line!
- **The Trap:** Thinking there is only one correct answer for $x$ and $y$. Emphasize that *every single point* lying on that straight line is a mathematically valid solution.

### 4. Simultaneous Equations: Concept & Graphing (20 min)
*What happens when two rules apply at the exact same time? Introduce a scenario like pricing chickens and ducks with two constraints.*
- **Strategies:** Form two equations like $x + y = 7$ and $2x + y = 12$. Use graphing software (like GeoGebra on the projector) to plot both lines. Show them the magic: the point where they cross (titik persilangan) is the unique solution!
- **The Trap:** Believing all simultaneous equations have one solution. explicitly show that parallel lines (selari) mean *no solution*, and perfectly overlapping lines (bertindih) mean *infinite solutions*.

### 5. Algebraic Methods: Substitution & Elimination (30 min)
*Now, teach the two primary algebraic techniques to find the crossing point without having to draw a graph every time.*
- **Strategies:** Teach *Kaedah Penggantian* (Substitution): isolating one variable and "inserting" it into the other equation. Teach *Kaedah Penghapusan* (Elimination): multiplying an equation so coefficients match, then stacking them to add/subtract a variable out of existence.
- **The Trap:** Adding equations incorrectly during Elimination. If they see $2y$ and $-2y$, students might subtract instead of adding. Teach them a catchy rule: "Same signs subtract, opposite signs add."

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Curriculum Guardrail (F1-06 vs F3-09):**
> In this chapter, students only need to understand that a linear equation forms a straight line and that the intersection is the simultaneous solution. **Do NOT** introduce the concepts of "gradient" ($m$), "intercepts" ($c$), or the formal equation $y = mx + c$. These are reserved for Form 3 (Garis Lurus). Keep the focus purely on point-plotting and algebraic manipulation at this stage.


## High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | How to Fix |
|---------------|-------------------------|------------|
| **Addition instead of Division:** $3x=15 \rightarrow x=15-3$ | Thinking 3 and $x$ are added together, rather than multiplied. | Explicitly read it aloud as "3 *times* x", so the mathematical opposite is division, not subtraction. |
| **Stopping Halfway:** Finding $x=4$ and stopping in simultaneous equations | Believing the problem is solved once they find the first variable. | Remind them that the solution is a coordinate on a graph representing the intersection $(x,y)$. They must substitute $x$ back in to find $y$. |
| **Sign Errors in Elimination:** $2x - (-3x) = -1x$ | Weak integer arithmetic during the vertical subtraction of the elimination method. | Practice stacking and subtracting negative terms beforehand. Make them circle the double negatives visually. |

## Engagement Hooks
- **Physical Scale:** Use a physical scale balance and identical weighted blocks to physically solve simple 1-variable equations right in front of the classroom by adding/removing blocks equally from both sides.
- **Two Truths and a Lie (Graphing Edition):** Show a graph with a line and three coordinate points $(x,y)$. Two points are actual solutions to the equation, one is a lie. Have them spot the lie by independently substituting the points.
- **Tech Integration:** Have students use GeoGebra on their phones to input simultaneous equations and physically pinch-zoom to pinpoint exactly where the lines cross to find the answer.

## Assessment Guidance
- Do not assess substitution and elimination methods blindly; require students to state *why* they chose a particular method for a specific pair of equations.
- Include word problems that force students to translate English/BM into mathematical equations before solving.
- Assess if they can identify the difference between equations with 1 unique solution, infinite solutions, and no solutions.
