# Ketaksamaan Linear (Linear Inequalities) - Teaching Notes

## Overview
Now that your students understand equations and the concept of balance, it's time to introduce scenarios where things are not equal! "Ketaksamaan" (Inequalities) explores the relationship between two quantities that do not have the same value. This chapter bridges their prior knowledge of the number line and linear equations to deal with ranges of possible values.

This topic is divided into two major DSKP standards that you'll guide them through:
*   **7.1 Ketaksamaan (Inequalities):** Understanding basic inequalities using symbols (<, >, ≤, ≥), representing them on a number line, and generalizing the properties of inequalities (converse, transitive, and the effects of basic arithmetic operations).
*   **7.2 Ketaksamaan Linear dalam Satu Pemboleh Ubah (Linear Inequalities in One Variable):** Forming inequalities from daily situations, solving single linear inequalities using algebraic operations, and solving simultaneous linear inequalities in one variable by finding their common overlapping region.

## Prerequisites Check
Before diving in, make sure your students have:
- A solid grasp of the number line, specifically understanding that values increase as you move to the right and decrease as you move to the left (e.g., knowing that −7 is less than −2).
- A strong foundation in basic arithmetic operations involving integers (from Chapter 1).
- Familiarity with forming algebraic expressions and solving simple linear equations using the balance method (as taught in Chapter 6), because solving linear inequalities uses an almost identical process.

## Teaching Sequence

### 1. Ketaksamaan (Inequalities) [DSKP 7.1]
**A. Recognising and Forming Inequalities (15 min)**
Start your lesson by putting a physical balancing scale on the board that is imbalanced (one side heavier than the other). Explain that this relationship is an inequality.
- Introduce the symbols: Use a number line to introduce "<" (less than / kurang daripada) and ">" (greater than / lebih besar daripada).
- Introduce limits: Expand to situations involving limits to introduce "≤" (less than or equal to) and "≥" (greater than or equal to). Show them a speed limit sign (e.g., 110 km/j) and guide them to understand that the speed v must be v≤110. Use voting age (21 years old and above) for t≥21.

**B. Properties of Inequalities (25 min)**
Run a quick exploration activity where students test numbers to see what happens to the inequality symbols. Teach them the critical properties:
- Sifat Akas (Converse Property): If a<b, then b>a. (e.g., If 2<5, then 5>2).
- Sifat Transitif (Transitive Property): If a<b<c, then a<c. (e.g., If −15<−8<0, then −15<0).
- Arithmetic Operations (The Golden Rule!): Show that adding or subtracting positive/negative numbers, or multiplying/dividing by positive numbers keeps the symbol the same.
- The Big Flip: Explicitly demonstrate that multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number, or finding the reciprocal, reverses the direction of the inequality symbol (e.g., if 2<6, multiplying by −1 makes it −2>−6).

### 2. Ketaksamaan Linear dalam Satu Pemboleh Ubah (Linear Inequalities in One Variable) [DSKP 7.2]
**A. Forming and Solving Single Inequalities (20 min)**
A linear inequality has a variable with a highest power of 1, and unlike equations, it has more than one possible solution. Give them keywords to translate into math: "At least/Minimum" = ≥, and "At most/Maximum" = ≤.
- Solving Process: Show them that solving inequalities is just like solving equations, moving terms to isolate the variable x.
- Crucial reminder during solving: Whenever they multiply or divide by a negative number to isolate x, they MUST flip the sign (e.g., −4x>8 becomes x<−2).

**B. Solving Simultaneous Linear Inequalities (20 min)**
What happens when a variable must satisfy two rules at the same time? Use the textbook example of healthy daily sugar intake (m>25 and m<37.5).
- Graphical Solving: Have students solve both inequalities separately. Then, have them draw a single number line and plot both solution ranges as arrows.
- The final answer is the "bahagian sepunya" (common part/intersection) where the two arrows overlap.

## Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Why Students Think This | How to Address |
|---------------|------------------------|----------------|
| Forgetting to flip the symbol when dividing/multiplying by a negative number. | They treat it exactly like an equation where the "=" sign never changes. | Continually reinforce the "Big Flip" rule. Use a simple test like 3>1, multiply by −1, and ask if −3>−1 is true. They will see why the sign must flip to −3<−1. |
| Confusing keywords "at least" with ≤ and "at most" with ≥. | The word "least" sounds like "less than" and "most" sounds like "greater than". | Translate it to conversational contexts. "To watch this movie, you must be at least 18." Ask them, "Can a 19-year-old watch? Can a 10-year-old?" This helps them intuitively realize it means ≥18. |
| Drawing incorrect open/closed circles on the number line. | They don't differentiate between strict inequalities (<, >) and inclusive ones (≤, ≥). | Explicitly teach that an empty circle (∘) is for < or > (does not include the number), and a solid/filled circle (∙) is for ≤ or ≥ (includes the number). |

## Engagement Hooks
- **Human Number Line**: Create a large number line on the floor using tape or a rope. Give two students different numbers to stand on and ask the class who is greater. Then, have them physically walk the steps to add or subtract a number, observing if the inequality relationship changes.
- **Real-World Limits Game**: Flash pictures of different real-world limit signs on a screen (e.g., a roller coaster height limit of 110cm, a bridge weight limit of 8T, a maximum capacity in an elevator of 960kg). Give the first student to correctly write the algebraic inequality on their whiteboard a point.
- **"Two Truths and a Lie" (Inequality Edition)**: Put up three solved simultaneous inequalities with their number lines. Two have correct overlapping regions, one has a logical flaw. Have the students spot the error.

## Assessment Guidance (Berdasarkan Tahap Penguasaan / TP)
Use the DSKP Standard Prestasi framework for Bab 7 to assess their learning progress:
- **TP 1**: Can they demonstrate basic knowledge of linear inequalities? (e.g., identifying the correct symbol <,> to compare two integers).
- **TP 2**: Can they demonstrate understanding of linear inequalities? (e.g., describing an inequality using a number line or stating the converse/transitive properties).
- **TP 3**: Can they apply their understanding to perform simple tasks? (e.g., forming a basic inequality from a daily situation or solving a direct 1-step linear inequality).
- **TP 4**: Can they apply knowledge to solve simple routine problems? (e.g., solving multi-step inequalities or basic simultaneous inequalities).
- **TP 5**: Can they solve complex routine problems? (e.g., extracting multiple conditions from a word problem, forming simultaneous inequalities, and finding the final range of values).
- **TP 6**: Can they apply their knowledge to non-routine problems creatively? (e.g., solving open-ended real-world optimization problems like the theme park profit task in the Eksplorasi Matematik section).
