# Bab 7: Ketaksamaan Linear (Linear Inequalities) - Teaching Notes

## Overview
Now that your students understand equations and the concept of balance, it's time to introduce scenarios where things are not equal! "Ketaksamaan" (Inequalities) explores the relationship between two quantities that do not have the same value. This chapter bridges their prior knowledge of the number line and linear equations to deal with ranges of possible values. It's a highly visual and logical topic that translates directly into everyday real-world limits like speeds, capacities, and ages.

## Prerequisites Check
- A solid grasp of the number line, specifically understanding that values increase as you move to the right and decrease to the left (F1-01 Nombor Nisbah).
- A strong foundation in basic arithmetic operations involving integers (F1-01 Nombor Nisbah).
- Familiarity with the equation balancing process (F1-06 Persamaan Linear), because solving linear inequalities uses an almost identical algebraic isolation process.

## Teaching Sequence & Strategy

### 1. Recognising and Forming Inequalities (15 min)
*Start your lesson by putting a physical balancing scale on the board that is visibly imbalanced (one side heavier). Explain that a Ketaksamaan is exactly this relationship!*
- **Strategies:** Expand directly to situations involving limits to introduce "<=" (less than or equal to) and ">=" (greater than or equal to). Show them a real speed limit sign (110 km/j) and guide them to $v \leq 110$. Use voting age (21) for $t \geq 21$.
- **The Trap:** Confusing the symbols $<$ and $>$. Remind them the "alligator mouth" always eats the bigger number, or use the number line where the arrow specifically points to the smaller values for $<$.

### 2. Properties of Inequalities (25 min)
*Run a quick exploration activity where students test numbers to physically see what happens to the symbols.*
- **Strategies:** Teach the properties explicitly: *Sifat Akas* (Converse): If $a<b$, then $b>a$. *Sifat Transitif* (Transitive): If $a<b$ and $b<c$, then $a<c$. For arithmetic operations, show that adding/subtracting keeps the symbol exactly the same.
- **The Trap:** The Big Flip! Explicitly demonstrate that multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number reverses the direction of the symbol (e.g., if $2<6$, multiplying by $-1$ makes it $-2>-6$ because $-2$ is larger than $-6$ on the number line).

### 3. Forming and Solving Single Inequalities (20 min)
*A linear inequality has a variable with a highest power of 1, and unlike equations, it represents an entire shaded range of possible solutions.*
- **Strategies:** Give them English/BM keywords to translate into math: "At least/Minimum" = $\geq$, and "At most/Maximum" = $\leq$. Tell them solving inequalities is just like solving equations - move terms to isolate $x$.
- **The Trap:** Whenever they multiply or divide by a negative number to isolate $x$ at the very last step, they MUST flip the sign (e.g., $-4x > 8$ becomes $x < -2$). Remind them constantly!

### 4. Solving Simultaneous Linear Inequalities (20 min)
*What happens when a variable must satisfy two rules at the exact same time? Use a clear textbook example, like healthy daily sugar intake ($m>25$ and $m<37.5$).*
- **Strategies:** Have students solve both inequalities separately. Then, have them draw a single number line horizontally and plot *both* solution ranges as arrows directly above it.
- **The Trap:** The final answer is strictly the "bahagian sepunya" (common part/intersection) where the two arrows vertically overlap. If they don't overlap, there is *no solution*.

## High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | How to Fix |
|---------------|-------------------------|------------|
| **Forgetting to Flip:** $ -2x < 10 \rightarrow x < -5 $ | They treat it exactly like an equation where the "$=$" sign never changes direction. | Continually reinforce the "Big Flip" rule. Use a simple test like $3 > 1$, multiply by $-1$, and ask if $-3 > -1$ is logically true. They will physically see why the sign must flip to $-3 < -1$. |
| **Keyword Confusion:** "At least" = $\leq$ | The word "least" sounds conceptually like "less than" ($\leq$), and "most" sounds like "greater than" ($\geq$). | Translate it to conversational contexts. "To watch this movie, you must be at least 18." Ask them, "Can a 19-year-old watch? A 10-year-old?" This intuitively proves it means $\geq 18$. |
| **Number Line Errors:** drawing $\circ$ instead of $\bullet$ | They don't differentiate between strict inequalities ($<, >$) and inclusive ones ($\leq, \geq$). | Explicitly teach that an empty circle ($\circ$) is for $<$ or $>$ (does not include the number), and a solid/filled circle ($\bullet$) is for $\leq$ or $\geq$ (includes the exact boundary). |

## Engagement Hooks
- **Human Number Line**: Create a large number line on the floor using masking tape. Give two students different numbers to stand on and ask the class who is mathematically "greater". Have them physically hop the steps to add or subtract a number while holding the inequality sign.
- **Real-World Limits Game**: Flash pictures of different real-world limit signs on the projector (e.g., a roller coaster height limit of 110cm, an elevator maximum capacity of 960kg, a bridge weight limit of 8T). First student to correctly write the algebraic inequality on their whiteboard gets a point!
- **"Two Truths and a Lie" (Inequality Edition)**: Put up three solved simultaneous inequalities with their drawn number lines. Two have correctly shaded overlapping regions, one has a logical flaw (e.g., shading outside the overlap). Have the students vote and spot the error.

## Assessment Guidance
- Always test their ability to graph their final answer on a number line, not just solve algebraically.
- Require students to list the integer values that satisfy an inequality (e.g., "List all possible integers $x$ where $-2 < x \leq 3$").
- Include questions that specifically test their alertness to The Big Flip (dividing a negative coefficient).
- Simultaneous inequality questions should test all three scenarios: bounded ranges ($2 < x < 5$), unbounded rays ($x > 3$), and the 'no solution' empty overlap.
