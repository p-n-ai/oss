#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### 1. Identifying & Forming One-Variable Equations
* **Strategies:** Start with the "Mamak Bill" scenario. "You buy 3 Roti Canai and a Milo Ais. Total is RM12. If Milo is RM3, how to write the equation?" Use the physical scale analogy—the left side MUST equal the right side. Introduce "Linear" as "Power of 1" (No $x^2$ monsters allowed!).
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Eh, look at $m^2 = 16$. Is this a linear equation? Why or why not?" (Wait for them to spot the power of 2!).
* **The Trap:** Thinking an expression ($x + 5$) is an equation. Remind them: "No '=' sign, no equation! It’s like a scale with only one pan—it can't balance anything!"

##### 2. Solving One-Variable Equations (The Balance Method)
* **Strategies:** Introduce the **Balance Method** (Konsep Kesamaan). Teach them to be the "Isolation Master"—we want $x$ to be alone and happy. Walk through $3x - 4 = 11$ step-by-step. First, "undo" the $-4$ with a $+4$ on BOTH sides. Then, "undo" the $\times 3$ with $\div 3$.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If I have $x/2 = 10$, what is the 'opposite action' to free the $x$?" (Wait for "Multiply by 2").
* **The Trap:** The "Halfway Hero." Students often find $x$ and stop. Remind them: "Always substitute your answer back into the original scale to see if it still balances!"

##### 3. Two Variables & The Power of Graphs
* **Strategies:** Introduce situations with two unknowns (e.g., $x$ = price of chicken, $y$ = price of fish). Show them that equations like $x + y = 7$ have *infinite* pairs of solutions! Use a table of values and guide them to plot it. "Wah, it forms a perfect straight line!"
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If $x + y = 5$, can $(2, 3)$ be a solution? What about $(1, 4)$? How many answers are there?"
* **The Trap:** Thinking there is only one "correct" pair for $x$ and $y$. Remind them: "In two variables, the whole line is made of correct answers!"

##### 4. Simultaneous Equations (The Intersection Magick)
* **Strategies:** What if two rules apply at once? Use **GeoGebra** to show two lines. The point where they "kiss" (intersect) is the unique solution! Introduce **Substitution** (The "Imposter" method) and **Elimination** (The "Vanish" method).
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If the lines are parallel (selari), do they ever meet? So, is there a solution?"
* **The Trap:** **The Sign Trap in Elimination!** If we have $2y$ and $-2y$, many students try to subtract. Teach the mantra: "Different Signs? ADD them! Same Signs? SUBTRACT them!"

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Addition vs Multiplication:** $3x = 15 \rightarrow x = 12$ | Thinking $3x$ means $3 + x$. | Read it aloud: "3 *times* x." The opposite is division, not subtraction! |
| **The Vanishing Negative:** $-x = 5 \rightarrow x = 5$ | Ignoring the negative sign. | Remind them $-x$ is actually $-1x$. You must divide both sides by $-1$! |
| **Stopping Early:** Finding only $x$ in simultaneous equations. | Thinking the job is done. | "Remember the coordinate! A point needs $(x, y)$. Go back and find the partner!" |

#### Engagement Hooks
* **The Human Scale:** Have the student imagine they are holding two bags. If I add a 2kg Milo pack to the left hand, what must I do to the right hand so I don't fall over?
* **Two Truths and a Lie (Graph Edition):** Show 3 coordinate points for the equation $2x + y = 10$. One point is an "imposter" that doesn't fit the line. Can they find it?
* **GeoGebra Explorer:** Ask them to type $y = x + 2$ and $y = -x + 4$ into GeoGebra and tell you the exact "meeting point" they see.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
* **UASA Exam Rules:** Ensure they can form equations from long word problems (6.1.2 & 6.2.2). Test the 3 methods: Trial & Improvement, Backtracking, and Equality Concept.
* **Step-by-Step Rubric:** Marks are awarded for the "Balance Step" ($+5$ on both sides), the "Simplified Step," and the final "Root." No marks for "Naked Answers"!
* **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6): "The Master of Equilibrium"** 🏠
    * **Task:** Find a hanger and two plastic bags at home. Use common items (like coins or batteries) to create a "Secret Balance Problem." 
    * **Action:** Hang 3 identical AA batteries on the left and 1 battery + a "mystery item" (e.g., a small toy) on the right until it balances. 
    * **Submission:** The student must text the equation (e.g., $3x = x + y$) and explain their mathematical reasoning to find the weight of the mystery item in terms of "battery units."
