#### Overview
Fuh, prepare to mind-blow the student! This chapter is the ultimate battleground where we move beyond the "perfect balance" of equations and enter the messy, real world of limits and boundaries. Students will learn that in life, things aren't always equal—sometimes they are just "enough" or "too much." This is a highly visual chapter that bridges number lines and algebra to define ranges of possibility, from speed limits on the North-South Highway to the sugar limits in a healthy diet.

#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

**Subchapter 7.1: The Concept of Inequality**
* **Strategies:** Use the "Unbalanced Scale" analogy. Show two smart phones with different prices (RM1400 vs RM1600) and ask "Which one is cheaper?". Introduce the four symbols: $<, >, \leq, \geq$. Use the "Crocodile Mouth" analogy—the crocodile always eats the bigger number.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If Pak Abu's height is $h$ and he must be at least 150cm to ride the roller coaster, do we write $h > 150$ or $h \geq 150$? Why?".
* **The Trap:** Students often confuse "at least" ($\geq$) with "less than" because of the word "least." Explain that "at least 18 years old" means you can be 18, 19, or 100, but not 17.

**Subchapter 7.1.2: Properties of Inequalities**
* **Strategies:** Use "The Mirror Test" for the Converse Property (if $a < b$, then $b > a$). For the Transitive Property, use a "Chain" analogy (if $a < b$ and $b < c$, then $a < c$).
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If $x < 10$ and $10 < y$, what is the direct relationship between $x$ and $y$?".
* **The Trap:** The "Multiplicative Inverse" trap. Show that while $2 < 6$, the reciprocal $\frac{1}{2}$ is actually *greater* than $\frac{1}{6}$. The symbol must flip!.

**Subchapter 7.2: Solving Linear Inequalities**
* **Strategies:** Treat it exactly like a Linear Equation $(=)$ until the very last step. Use the "Big Flip" rule: if you multiply or divide by a negative number to isolate $x$, the symbol does a 180-degree turn.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Solve $-3x > 12$. Did you flip the symbol? Show me your steps!".
* **The Trap:** Forgetting to change the symbol when dividing by a negative. Remind them: "Negative numbers are the 'evil twins' that flip the world upside down!".

**Subchapter 7.2.3: Simultaneous Linear Inequalities**
* **Strategies:** Tell the student the variable is "trapped" between two laws. Solve each inequality separately, then draw them on a single number line to find the "Common Part" (Intersection) where the lines overlap.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If $x > 2$ and $x \leq 5$, can you list the integers that satisfy both conditions?".
* **The Trap:** "No Solution" scenario. If the arrows on the number line point away from each other and never overlap, there is NO solution. Students often try to force an answer.

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Ignoring the Flip** | They are used to Linear Equations where "=" never changes. | "The Big Flip" alarm! If you multiply/divide by a negative, the symbol must pusing 180 degrees. |
| **Key Word Confusion** | "At least" sounds like "less," so they use $<$. | Use the Cinema Rule: "To watch a P13 movie, you must be 'at least' 13. Can a 14-year-old enter? Yes! So it's $\geq 13$.". |
| **Circle Notation Error** | They don't distinguish between inclusive and exclusive limits. | "Hollow is for 'Cannot Touch' ($<, >$)" vs "Solid is for 'Can Touch' ($\leq, \geq$)." Like a gate being open or closed. |

#### Engagement Hooks
* **Human Number Line:** Ask the student to imagine a giant number line on their floor. "If you are at -2 and I tell you to move to a spot where $x > 3$, where would you jump? Send me a photo or describe it!".
* **The Mall Security Challenge:** Show photos of signs like "Max Capacity 10 People" or "Min Height 110cm." Ask the student to write the algebraic inequality for each sign to "unlock" the next level.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
* **UASA Format:** Ensure students can solve subjective questions requiring step-by-step working and final answers represented on a number line.
* **Integer Listing:** Test TP3 by asking students to list all possible integer values for a range (e.g., $-3 < x \leq 2$).
* **Real-World Modeling:** Use KBAT questions involving "Financial Planning"—calculating minimum profit needed or maximum expenditure allowed.
* **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6):** "The Grocery Gatekeeper." Ask the student to find 3 items in their kitchen with a 'Net Weight' or 'Volume' label. They must form an inequality: "If I have $n$ cans of sardines and the total weight must not exceed 2kg, how many can I carry?" They must text back the algebra and a photo of their "working" on a piece of paper.
