# Data Handling -- Teaching Notes

## Overview
Fuh, prepare to mind-blow the student. Chapter 12 is where everyday information becomes something we can collect, sort, display, and question with confidence. We are not just making pretty graphs; we are training the student to spot patterns, compare choices, and avoid being fooled by a misleading display.

## Prerequisites Check
* Basic arithmetic, especially fractions and percentages.
* Familiarity with measurement units such as kg, cm, and min.
* Comfort with simple plotting on a graph or table.

## Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### 12.1.1 Generate Statistical Questions and Collect Relevant Data
* **Strategies:** Start with a local context such as favorite food, screen time, or transport to school. Ask the student to decide whether the question will produce varied answers. Use a simple survey or class poll to collect data.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If I ask everyone their height, is that a statistical question? Why?"
* **The Trap:** Students write questions that have only one possible answer. No variability, no statistics.

##### 12.1.2 Classify Data and Construct Frequency Tables
* **Strategies:** Use a mamak menu or sports survey. Sort items into categorical or numerical data, then build a frequency table from the results.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Is the mass of a student categorical or numerical? Is it discrete or continuous?"
* **The Trap:** Students confuse categorical data with numerical data, or they think numerical data must always be whole numbers.

##### 12.1.3 Construct Ungrouped Data Representations
* **Strategies:** Show the same data in bar charts, pie charts, line graphs, dot plots, and stem-and-leaf plots. Ask the student to choose the most suitable one and explain why.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If we want to show height changes from age 5 to age 13, which graph is most suitable?"
* **The Trap:** Students choose a graph just because it looks nice, not because it matches the data type or purpose.

##### 12.1.4 Convert One Representation to Another
* **Strategies:** Give the student a bar chart and ask them to convert it into a line graph or frequency table. Keep the original values unchanged while changing the form.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Can a stem-and-leaf plot become a dot plot without losing the original data values?"
* **The Trap:** Students convert the data but accidentally change the meaning or lose the original numbers.

##### 12.1.5 Interpret Data and Make Inferences
* **Strategies:** Use real examples such as rainfall, social media likes, or student attendance. Teach the student to read trends, compare groups, and make cautious predictions.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "What pattern do you notice? What might happen next if the trend continues?"
* **The Trap:** Students read only the numbers and ignore the trend, scale, or interval meaning.

##### 12.1.6 Represent Data Ethically
* **Strategies:** Show a misleading graph with a broken y-axis or inconsistent scale and ask the student to critique it. Then redraw it properly.
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If a pie chart totals only 96%, can we trust it? What happened to the missing 4%?"
* **The Trap:** Students think any graph is fine as long as it is colourful. Ethical representation matters more than decoration.

## High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Numerical data must be big numbers only | They equate numerical with large | Show temperatures, times, and masses with decimals |
| Any graph suits any data | They focus on appearance, not purpose | Match graph choice to the question and data type |
| A graph is accurate if it looks neat | They trust presentation over scale | Check axis scale, labels, and totals before accepting it |

## Engagement Hooks
* Use a class survey on favourite snacks, sports, or phone apps.
* Track weather or rainfall data from a local news source.
* Analyse social media likes or game scores over time.

## Assessment Guidance
* Expect students to justify why they selected a particular representation.
* Check that they can identify categorical versus numerical data.
* Ask them to critique a misleading graph and redraw it ethically.
* Include a TP6 mini project where they collect home or class data, build a table, and present it with a graph.

## Chatbot Projek Mini
* **The Media Mogul Challenge:** Ask the student to collect data on three items at home, such as sugar content in drinks or time spent on homework.
* They must build a frequency table, choose one suitable graph, and explain why the graph is fair and easy to read.
* They must also check the y-axis start point and confirm that the graph does not mislead the reader.
