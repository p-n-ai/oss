#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### Introduction to Quadratic Expressions (Textbook p. 2-5)
* **Strategies:** Use the "Highest Power Hunt." Ask the student to find the "King" of the expression—if the highest power is 2, it's a Quadratic!
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Look at $3x^3 + 2x^2 - 1$. Is this a quadratic? Why or why not?"
* **The Trap:** The "Hidden Zero" trap. Students think $x^2 + 5$ isn't a quadratic because there's no $x$ term. Remind them $b$ can be zero!

##### Functions & Many-to-One Relations (Textbook p. 5-10)
* **Strategies:** Use the "Vertical Line Test" analogy. Imagine a laser beam dropping down—if it hits two points on the $x$-axis that give the same $y$, it's a many-to-one relationship!
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If $f(2) = 4$ and $f(-2) = 4$, what kind of relation is this?"
* **The Trap:** Confusing the $x$-intercepts (roots) with the $y$-intercept ($c$).

##### The "Shape-Shifter" (Effects of a, b, c) (Textbook p. 11-14)
* **Strategies:** GeoGebra visualization. Show how increasing $a$ makes the graph "slim" and decreasing it makes it "fat."
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If I change $a=2$ to $a=0.5$, does the graph get wider or narrower?"
* **The Trap:** Thinking $c$ changes the shape. No lah! $c$ is just an elevator—it only moves the graph up and down!

##### Roots & Factorization (Textbook p. 16-22)
* **Strategies:** The "Ground Zero" strategy. Roots are where the graph touches the floor (x-axis).
* **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Why do we set $f(x)=0$ to find the roots?"
* **The Trap:** The "Sign Flip" error. Students factor to $(x-5)(x+2)=0$ but forget to flip the signs for the actual roots $x=5, x=-2$.

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Any expression with $x$ is quadratic | They ignore the power index | The "Power of 2" Rule. No 2, no Quadratic! |
| $c$ affects the width | They see the graph move and get confused | The "Elevator Analogy." $c$ only goes Up/Down. |
| Roots must be integers | Most textbook examples use whole numbers | Show them a graph where the root is 1.5. |

#### Engagement Hooks
* **The Free Kick Master:** Ask the student to imagine Safawi Rasid taking a free kick. The ball's path isn't a straight line—it's a parabola!
* **Bridge Architect:** Show the Putrajaya bridge. Why is it curved? To distribute weight! That curve is a quadratic function.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
* **UASA Exam Rules:** Ensure students can write equations in general form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ before factorizing.
* **UASA Exam Rules:** Practice sketching graphs with three labeled points: Shape, $y$-intercept, and Roots.
* **UASA Exam Rules:** Focus on real-world area problems (e.g., area of a rectangular garden).
* **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6):** **"The Kitchen Parabola."** Ask the student to find a curved object at home (a bowl, a lamp, or even the spray from a tap). Tell them to take a photo and "trace" it on a piece of graph paper. They must then estimate where the "axis of symmetry" is and text back their reasoning.
