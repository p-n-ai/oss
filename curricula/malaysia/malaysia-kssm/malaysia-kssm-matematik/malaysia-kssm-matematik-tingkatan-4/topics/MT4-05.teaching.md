#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### Subchapter 5.1: Network Concepts
*   **Strategies:** Use the **KL Sentral Transit Map** analogy. Vertices ($V$) are the stations, and Edges ($E$) are the train tracks connecting them. Introduce the Sum of Degrees formula: $\sum d(v) = 2E$.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If I have 5 stations and 4 tracks, what is the total sum of degrees? Calculate now, don't simply guess!"
*   **The Trap:** The "Loop Degree" Trap! A loop starts and ends at the same vertex, so it counts as **2 degrees**. If the student says it's 1, they will *kantoi* on the sum of degrees formula!

##### Comparing Graph Types (Directed vs Weighted)
*   **Strategies:** Contrast a **Social Media Follower** list (Directed - you follow them, they might not follow you) vs. a **Friendship** list (Undirected). For Weighted graphs, use **Grab/Foodpanda delivery prices** as the "weight" on the edges.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If the edge between Point A and B represents 'Time taken', is this a weighted or unweighted graph?"
*   **The Trap:** Students often ignore the arrows in directed graphs when calculating $d_{in}$ and $d_{out}$. Force them to draw small arrows pointing "In" and "Out" to count correctly.

##### Subgraphs and Trees
*   **Strategies:** Teach that a **Tree** is like the most "efficient" friendship group—everyone is connected, but there's NO drama (no loops, no cycles). It must have $n-1$ edges.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "I have a graph with 10 vertices and 10 edges. Is this a tree? Why or why not?"
*   **The Trap:** The "Sub-Not-Subgraph" Trap. A subgraph MUST use the original vertices and edges. You cannot "invent" a new connection that wasn't in the main graph!

##### Problem Solving & Optimization
*   **Strategies:** Use **Algorithm Dijkstra-style logic** for shortest paths. Guide the student to compare multiple routes (e.g., Johor Bahru to Kota Bharu) by summing weights (time, cost, distance).
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Route P is shorter in distance but takes longer time. Why? Look at the 'weighted' context!"
*   **The Trap:** The "First Choice" Bias. Students often pick the first path they see. Force them to list at least 3 alternative routes before deciding on the "Best" one.

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Loops count as 1 degree | It's only one "line" or circle. | Show them that the line "leaves" the vertex and "enters" it again—that's two touches! |
| Every graph is a tree | They think "Tree" just means "Connected". | "No Drama Rule!" If you can travel in a circle and end up back where you started, it is NOT a tree. |
| Weights are only distances | Distance is the most common example in textbooks. | Remind them that weights can be money (RM), time (minutes), or even data usage (GB). |

#### Engagement Hooks
*   **The Viral Maze:** Show a maze and reveal that it's actually just a network of nodes. If you can turn a maze into a tree, you can solve it every time!
*   **Six Degrees of Separation:** Discuss how every person in the world is connected to a celebrity through a network of just 6 people.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
*   **UASA Rule 1:** Expect objective questions (OAP) on identifying subgraphs—watch out for "illegal" edges!
*   **UASA Rule 2:** Subjective questions often ask to "Draw a tree with minimum weight." Student must show the step-by-step removal of the heaviest edges.
*   **UASA Rule 3:** Representation tasks (TP4) will provide a table of data (e.g., game preferences) and ask for a network drawing.
*   **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6):** **"Map Your Social Circle!"** Ask the student to draw a network of their 5 closest friends. 
    *   *Rule:* Use Directed arrows if the "tagging" is one-way.
    *   *Challenge:* Turn that social circle into a "Tree" (remove edges until there are no cycles). The student must photo and upload their drawing, then explain which "friendship edge" was the "heaviest" to remove!
