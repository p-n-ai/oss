#### Overview
Fuh, prepare to mind-blow the student! This chapter is the ultimate battleground where we bridge human speech to mathematical precision. **Logical Reasoning** is the "brain" of computer programming and legal debates. We are teaching the student how to think like a coder and a lawyer at the same time!

> [!IMPORTANT] **Chatbot Delivery Rules:**
> * **Bite-Sized Pacing:** Max 2 short paragraphs per message. Never dump a whole truth table at once!
> * **Tone & Dwibahasa:** Use high-energy, relatable "Manglish" for delivery (e.g., "Ready to be a detective? Jom!").
> * **Golden Rule 1:** Always force the student to identify the "P" (antecedent) and "Q" (consequent) before they try to write an implication.
> * **Golden Rule 2:** If they get a truth value wrong, ask them for a "Counter-example" immediately to prove why.

#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### 3.1 Statements (3 sessions)
*   **Strategies:** Use "The Truth Police" game. Give them a sentence like "Let's play!" and ask if it has a definite "True" or "False" label. Introduce quantifiers "all" and "some" using circles on paper.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Is 'Go to the shop' a statement? Why or why not?"
*   **The Trap:** **The Conversational Trap.** Students think questions or commands are statements because they are "important." Remind them: No Truth Value = Not a Statement!

##### 3.2 Implications and Counter-examples (2 sessions)
*   **Strategies:** Force them to draw an arrow from Antecedent $\rightarrow$ Consequent. To teach Converse/Inverse/Contrapositive, use a 4-color card system to swap "P" and "Q" positions.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If I swap P and Q, what is that called? Akas (Converse) or Songsangan (Inverse)?"
*   **The Trap:** **The Counter-Example Panic.** Students think they need a complex math proof to negate "All polygons have diagonals." Tell them: "Just find ONE rebel shape (the triangle) to break the law!".

##### 3.3 Arguments: Deductive & Inductive (3 sessions)
*   **Strategies:** Use the "General $\rightarrow$ Specific" funnel for Deductive and the "Specific $\rightarrow$ General" ladder for Inductive. For Validity, use the **Form I, II, and III** templates strictly.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "I saw 3 white crows, so all crows are white. Is this a strong argument? Why?".
*   **The Trap:** **The "True but Invalid" Trap.** A conclusion can be true ("The Earth is round") but the argument structure is broken. Force them to check the "Form" first, then the "Truth."

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| "p or q" is only true if one is true, not both. | Daily use of "or" is usually exclusive (Tea or Coffee). | **The Mamak Rule:** If you order "Roti or Milo," and the waiter brings both, he didn't lie! Logical "OR" includes "BOTH". |
| A valid argument is always "Sound" (Munasabah). | They ignore that Premise 1 might be a lie. | **The Alien Trap:** "All aliens eat glass. Zim is an alien. Therefore Zim eats glass." This is VALID but UNSOUND because Premise 1 is fake. |
| Converse has the same truth value as the Statement. | They assume if "If it rains, the floor is wet" is true, the reverse is also true. | Use a **Sprinkler example:** The floor can be wet because of a sprinkler, not just rain!. |

#### Engagement Hooks
*   **The Parliament Debate:** Show them the picture of Parliament. Explain that every law is a series of "If-Then" implications that must be debated for "Truth Values."
*   **The Aristotle Connection:** Mention Aristotle (384 BC) used logic as a "technique for debating." It’s an ancient superpower for winning arguments!.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
*   **UASA Rule 1:** Ensure questions test the difference between "Valid" (Sah) and "Sound" (Munasabah) using Form I/II/III.
*   **UASA Rule 2:** Objective questions should focus on the truth table of "and/or" and identifying the "Contrapositive".
*   **UASA Rule 3:** Solving problems must involve the 4-step cycle: Understand, Plan, Implement, Check.
*   **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6):** **"The Detective's Alibi."** Ask the student to write three premises about their "whereabouts" last night (one must be a general statement, one specific). Then, they must text a Deductive Argument that "proves" they are innocent. They must use household items (like a clock or a receipt) and take a photo as "Evidence" for their premises.
