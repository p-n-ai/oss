#### Overview
Fuh, prepare to mind-blow the student! This chapter isn't just about math; it's about the physics of history-making speed! We are looking at how our national heroes like Mohamad Ridzuan Puzi and Azizulhasni Awang move through time and space. We will bridge the gap between simple speed calculations from Form 2 and the abstract beauty of gradients and areas under graphs. 

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Chatbot Delivery Rules:**
> 1. **Bite-Sized Pacing:** Never drop a full graph explanation at once. Break it into: Plotting -> Gradient -> Interpretation.
> 2. **The "Wait for Units" Rule:** Before allowing a student to calculate, always ask: "Steady bro, are your units in sync? Check if you need to convert minutes to hours first!".
> 3. **The "Graph ID" Check:** Every time a new graph appears, force the student to identify it: "Is this a Distance-Time or a Speed-Time graph? Don't 'kantoi' here!".

#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

**7.1 Distance-Time Graphs**
*   **Strategies:** 
    1. Direct the student to check the vertical axis label first—if it says 'Distance', the gradient is the speed!.
    2. Use the "Azreen cycling" example to show how a table of training data transforms into a visual journey.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "If the line is flat (horizontal) in this Distance-Time graph, did Azreen keep cycling, or is he just chilling (stationary) at the side of the road?".
*   **The Trap:** The 'Negative Gradient' fear. Tell them: "Negative gradient doesn't mean negative speed, bro; it just means the object is heading back to the origin (home)!".

**7.2 Speed-Time Graphs**
*   **Strategies:**
    1. Use the "Speedometer needle" visualization—how fast the needle moves up or down is the gradient (acceleration).
    2. Guide the student to physically "shade" the area under the graph to see how it represents the total distance traveled.
*   **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "The gradient here isn't speed anymore... it's the rate of change of speed. What do we call that in the racing world? (Acceleration)!".
*   **The Trap:** The 'Flat Line Confusing'. In a Speed-Time graph, a flat line means the object is ZOOMING at a 'Uniform Speed' (*Laju Seragam*), not stopped!.

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Horizontal line = Stopped | They see the line not going 'up' anymore and assume no movement. | Remind them: Check the Y-axis! If Y is speed and it's 80 km/h, the car is still moving at a constant speed!. |
| Negative gradient = Backwards | Confused with the direction change in distance-time graphs. | In Speed-Time, a negative gradient just means you're hitting the brakes (*Nyahpecutan*). |
| Minute/Hour Mismatch | They forget that $1 \text{ hour} = 60 \text{ minutes}$ in speed calculations. | Enforce the 'Passar Malam Hour' rule: If the graph is in minutes but the question asks for km/h, you MUST divide by 60 first!. |

#### Engagement Hooks
*   **The World Record Sprint:** Analyze Mohamad Ridzuan Puzi's 100m T36 world record. How would his graph look if he reached top speed at 5 seconds and maintained it?.
*   **The 'Pocket Rocketman' Challenge:** Azizulhasni Awang in track cycling. Use his speed-time graph to find the total distance of one lap by calculating the area of a trapezium.
*   **Public Transport Data:** Use a flight schedule from KLIA2 to Miri (2 hours 20 mins) to estimate the flight distance using an average speed graph.

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
*   **UASA Exam Rules:**
    1. Always show the formula for gradient ($m = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}$) or average speed to secure 'Planning' marks.
    2. Units are the ultimate killer; check for $km \ h^{-1}$ vs $m \ s^{-1}$ and convert before solving.
    3. For TP5/TP6, students must follow the 4-step problem-solving cycle: Understand, Plan, Solve, Verify.
*   **Chatbot Projek Mini (TP6 Creative):**
    "The GrabCar Challenge": Ask the student to note the distance and duration of their next car trip or bus ride. They must:
    1. Sketch a Distance-Time graph for that trip.
    2. Identify one stationary period (traffic light/stop) and calculate the total average speed.
    3. Send a photo of their hand-drawn graph or a screenshot from a digital tool like GeoGebra.
