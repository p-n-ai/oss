# Sukatan Kecenderungan Memusat — Teaching Notes

## Overview
In this topic, students will explore central tendency measures such as mode, mean, and median, and learn how to apply them to both ungrouped and grouped data. This includes understanding their implications and selecting the appropriate measure based on data characteristics, including the presence of outliers.

## Prerequisites Check
Students should already be familiar with:

- Understanding of data types: qualitative vs. quantitative.
- Simple data collection and organization techniques.

## Teaching Sequence & Strategy

### 1. Introduction to Central Tendency (15 min)
Begin by discussing the concept of central tendency and its importance in data analysis.
- **Strategies:** Use simple examples like students' scores in a recent test.
- **CFU:** Ask, "What do you think is the most common score in our test?"
- **The Trap:** Confusing mode with most frequently occurring number.

### 2. Calculating Mode, Mean, and Median (25 min)
Guide students through the calculation of mode, mean, and median for ungrouped data.
- **Strategies:** Use number cards to visually group data; employ a calculator for mean.
- **CFU:** "If we add a high score to our data, how will it affect the mean?"
- **The Trap:** Forgetting to divide by the correct number when calculating mean.

### 3. Grouped Data Analysis (30 min)
Discuss constructing frequency tables and determining central tendencies for grouped data.
- **Strategies:** Provide data sets; use graph paper for visual representation.
- **CFU:** "How do we find the median class in grouped data?"
- **The Trap:** Mistaking modal class for actual mode value.

## High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception               | Why Students Think This                                | How to Fix                                      |
|-----------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Mode is the same as mean    | Both are measures of central tendency                 | Use clear, distinct examples to show differences|
| Median is always the middle | Misunderstanding of ordered set requirement            | Emphasize ordering the data before finding median|
| Mean is unaffected by outliers| Lack of understanding of average calculation         | Demonstrate with examples showing outlier impact|

## Engagement Hooks
- Use locally relevant contexts like average rainfall, popular foods, or sports scores.
- Discuss how businesses use these measures to make decisions, like setting prices.

## Assessment Guidance
- Use a mix of multiple-choice and short-answer questions to assess understanding.
- Include real-life scenarios where students must decide which measure is most appropriate and justify their choice.
