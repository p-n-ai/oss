#### Overview
Prepare to blow the student’s mind! This chapter turns uncertainty into precision. We are decoding chance itself — from coin flips to real-world predictions like weather, games, and even decision-making in finance. **Simple Probability (Kebarangkalian Mudah)** is where mathematics becomes the language of “how likely.”

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Chatbot Delivery Rules:**
> * Bite-sized pacing only (max 2–3 short paragraphs per response).
> * Use energetic tutor persona (“Alright let’s crack this!”, “Fuhh this one interesting!”).
> * Always force students to identify $n(S)$ before $n(A)$.
> * Probability MUST always stay within $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$ — stop immediately if violated.
> * Use visual reasoning (lists, tables, mental images, tree diagrams).

**Topic Golden Rules:**
* Always define Sample Space before computing probability.
* Always simplify probability as a fraction first before converting to decimal/percent.

#### DSKP Anchors & Taxonomy
* **13.1 Experimental Probability:** Perform experiments and express probability as frequency over trials.
* **13.2 Probability Theory:** Define sample space and events, construct models, and compute $P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}$.
* **13.3 Complement Events:** Understand $P(A') = 1 - P(A)$.
* **13.4 Problem Solving:** Apply probability in real-world routine and non-routine contexts.

**Performance Levels (TP1–TP6):**
TP1: Basic knowledge of sample space  
TP2: Understand event relationships  
TP3: Apply basic probability  
TP4: Routine problem solving  
TP5: Complex routine problems  
TP6: Non-routine creative problem solving

#### Teaching Sequence & Strategy

##### 13.1 Experimental Probability — “The Real Experiment Lab”
* **Strategies:** Make students simulate coin flips (or virtual dice). Record results in a table after 10, 50, 100 trials. Highlight convergence toward 0.5.
* **CFU:** “If heads = 7 out of 10, is probability exactly 0.5? Why does it differ?”
* **The Trap:** Students assume experimental probability must equal theoretical probability immediately. Teach “law of large numbers” intuition.

---

##### 13.2 Sample Space & Events — “The Universe of Possibilities”
* **Strategies:** Build sample space using tree diagrams and listing methods.
* Example: choosing ice cream (vanilla/chocolate/strawberry) × topping (yes/no).
* **CFU:** “List ALL outcomes. Did you miss any combination?”
* **The Trap:** Students forget compound outcomes or duplicate entries.

---

##### 13.3 Complement Events — “The NOT Logic”
* **Strategies:** Use real-life contrast: rain vs not rain, pass vs fail.
* Always anchor with: $P(A') = 1 - P(A)$
* **CFU:** “If P(rain) = 0.3, what is P(no rain)?”
* **The Trap:** Students try to recompute complement manually instead of subtracting from 1.

---

##### 13.4 Problem Solving — “Probability in the Real World”
* **Strategies:** Mix exam-style + real-life (sports, school events, shopping draws).
* Train 4-step method:
  1. Understand
  2. Identify sample space
  3. Compute probability
  4. Verify reasonableness
* **The Trap:** Students skip defining sample space and jump straight to formula.

---

#### High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception | Why Students Think This | Localized Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Probability can exceed 1 | Misplacing numerator/denominator | “Total outcomes (S) is the universe — you cannot exceed it.” |
| Experimental = exact | Small sample size confusion | “Small data lies, big data reveals truth.” |
| Forgetting sample space | Jumping straight to formula | “No S, no solution.” |

#### Assessment Guidance & Projek Mini
* **UASA Rules:**
  - Always show sample space listing
  - Always write formula $P(A)=\frac{n(A)}{n(S)}$
  - Must interpret final probability (fraction → meaning)

* **TP6 Mini Project — “Home Probability Lab”**
  Ask student to:
  1. Collect 10–20 household objects (coins, pens, buttons)
  2. Define an event (e.g., picking a red item)
  3. Compute theoretical + experimental probability
  4. Compare results and explain differences
