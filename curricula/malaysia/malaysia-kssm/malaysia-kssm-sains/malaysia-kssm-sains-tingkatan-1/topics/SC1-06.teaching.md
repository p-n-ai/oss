## Prerequisites Check
Students should have a foundational understanding of atoms and molecules and be able to differentiate between elements and compounds. Familiarity with basic lab safety and equipment usage will be beneficial.

## Teaching Sequence & Strategy

### 1. Introduction to Atoms and Elements (15 min)
- **Teaching instructions:** Emphasize that all matter is composed of atoms, which are the building blocks. Use analogies like Lego bricks to explain how atoms form larger structures.
- **Strategies:** Incorporate physical models of atoms and molecules and use visual aids like diagrams.
- **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "What makes up everything around us?"
- **The Trap:** Confusing atoms with molecules; emphasize that atoms are singular units while molecules are combinations.

### 2. Distinguishing Elements and Compounds (20 min)
- **Teaching instructions:** Explain the difference between elements and compounds using everyday examples.
- **Strategies:** Create a collaborative chart with students to classify common substances as elements or compounds.
- **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Can you name a compound you use daily?"
- **The Trap:** Mistaking mixtures for compounds; highlight the chemical bonding in compounds.

### 3. Navigating the Periodic Table (25 min)
- **Teaching instructions:** Help students explore the periodic table, focusing on groups like metals, non-metals, and noble gases.
- **Strategies:** Utilize interactive periodic table apps or classroom posters for engagement.
- **Check for Understanding (CFU):** "Where would you find metals on the periodic table?"
- **The Trap:** Not recognizing periodic trends; use color coding to differentiate groups.

## High Alert Misconceptions
| Misconception                   | Why Students Think This                      | How to Fix                                       |
|---------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| Atoms and molecules are the same | Both are small and invisible                 | Use models to show atoms as single, molecules as combinations |
| All shiny things are metals     | Shiny appearance is associated with metals   | Provide examples of shiny non-metals like iodine |

## Engagement Hooks
- Use local examples, such as tin (an important resource in Malaysia) to discuss the periodic table and its applications.
- Discuss the role of elements in technology, like silicon in electronics.

## Assessment Guidance
- Use quizzes that require students to place elements in the correct group on a blank periodic table.
- Encourage students to explain the difference between an element and a compound in their own words.
